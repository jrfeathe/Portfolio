### 1) Angry client / conflict

1. What’s your first move if a client is angry? (e.g., acknowledge/empathize/ask clarifying questions)

  I try to understand their perspective and determine root cause. Then acknowledge impact, de-escalate, and propose a path forward. It is good to have a confirmed plan to alleviate frustration. 

2. Do you prefer a quick call, or async response first?

  I almost always prefer async. Calling is useful in certain situations, but I think most problems can be solved perfectly fine async.

3. How do you de‑escalate while still holding boundaries?

  Using tactful speech and empathy. Try not to assume things about the person, yet develop a basic understanding of their perspective.

4. When do you escalate internally?

  Escalation is warranted when the problem has a negative impact on others, or is a problem that impacts one other person cannot be solved healthily man-to-man.

### 2) Ambiguity & requirements

5. How do you handle unclear requirements?

  For something unclear it is situational. Sometimes it is best to empathize with a product owner and try to take a best guess, then show them your prototype. Other times it is best to try clarifying first before doing any work. 

6. What do you do if the client changes scope mid‑stream?

  If scope changes then we need to emphasize that the budget and timeline need re-evaluation. If there are existing planning documents, we need to take a step back and generate new documents that pivot the scope. We can't immediately begin working in a new scope without a plan, new budget, and new timeline to release.

7. How do you document decisions/tradeoffs?

  It is usually appropriate to simply jot some things down in a document. For a large decision, document the current context and the response to how this will be handled. For a tradeoff, list the pros and cons, and describe their weights on the decision. This doesn't have to be made too complicated. 

### 3) Deadlines & quality

8. How do you communicate risk to a deadline?

  Document the deadline and all the tasks that still need to be completed. Provide estimates for the current workload, including the scope and resulting timeline of each task. Show how the timelines may overrun the deadline, and make it clear to see what scope items are bloating the deadline.

9. When would you cut scope vs push date?

  It is appropriate to cut scope for non-critical features. It is quite situational if we are cutting scope for quality. Some quality-of-life features may take less priority over others. If a product is bad, we should push the deadline. If a product is already decent but needs some fixes, it may be appropriate to cut scope. It is good to review the common use cases for the product to determine what is most critical.

10. What’s your default tradeoff: speed vs quality vs clarity?

  Speed and Quality are a balancing act. Clarity is always important, but at a certain point its improvement has diminishing returns. This is a triangle of balance, and it is never appropriate to dump one entirely. I think I tend to weigh quality a bit higher than speed. I view clarity as somewhat part of quality.

### 4) Team collaboration

11. Preferred collaboration style: async‑first, sync‑heavy, or hybrid?

  I really love async. I don't mind synchronous meetings and view them as necessary sometimes, especially at the start of a project. But async is the most comfortable once a workflow is set.

12. Meeting cadence you like (standups, weekly check‑ins, etc.)?

  I'm totally happy to provide a summary of my work every few days; it's actually quite good for me. I like to see how much I have accomplished each week. Every day gets a bit excessive, but is still fine. I think every two or three days is perfect. Weekly summaries of my work tend to be concise but lengthy and I feel nobody else is reading them.

13. How do you keep teammates aligned (docs, notes, demos, etc.)?

  I think documentation is really important. It lets team members jump into a feature with appropriate context. We ideally want a few people able to work on each feature, even if one person mostly owns a feature, each person doesn't understand every feature, or they have certain specialities. Having cemented plans is also really important. Git commits having Task or Epic references in naming conventions is a great help. Having a task list / scrum board / WBS is a huge help for organization.

14. How do you handle disagreements with a teammate or PM?

  It is usually best to take a step back before reacting. If we disagree over a feature within the project, come to a short term agreement. Take a step back and compile evidence or reasoning. Present the reasoning with appropriate demeanor at an appropriate time. Be prepared to sacrifice your own goals for the application if the Project Manager / higher-up gives direction to. Disagreements between teammates should be handled personally unless they are unhealthy for workflow, the team, or the whole project. It is good to compile evidence and align principles according to the decision-maker of the group, then commit to the chosen path.

### 5) Leadership & mentoring

15. What size team do you feel comfortable leading?

  I can lead a team of about 8 comfortably. Having a co-leader for larger teams would be a huge stress reliever. For a team of about twenty, one co-leader would be great. For a team of fifty I would probably be comfortable leading in a group of four or five leaders.

16. How do you support junior teammates?

  I try to be encouraging that they can solve a problem, rather than trying to solve it myself. I may give some pointers or additional context if I have any. If a junior really needs help, I'm happy to jump in a problem with them and come to a solution together. Digging a path forward to orient them can help a lot.

17. How do you give/receive feedback?

  I tend to give positive feedback quite often, but I often withhold negative feedback unless I deem it necessary to discuss. It is best to be tactful and constructive when giving negative feedback, and avoid insulting the skills / capabilities of the person. 

### 6) Ethics & confidentiality

18. If asked to “keep a secret,” what’s your boundary? (e.g., legal/ethical issues, transparency)

  I respect confidentiality unless there are safety/legal/ethical concerns; and encourage transparency. I don't like keeping secrets, even constructive ones. Benign things like "I'm secretly attending a class for _" are easy to withhold. Harmful secrets call ethics into question, and it is really a case-by-case basis. If someone will get hurt, then it is always best to report. If the secret only hurts me, then I may avoid reporting. Transparency is a great policy so I tend to always encourage it.

19. What privacy or data‑handling principles matter most to you?

  I really value privacy and I don't like how data is being sold on the internet. Advertisements have been so creepy. Nothing is private anymore. I think widespread AI integration and cloud handling of data is a recipe for serious privacy violations. Action should be taken to limit the damage. With quantum computing an unforeseen level of encryption-cracking is coming, making air-gapping more important than ever.

20. What ethical lines are non‑negotiable?

  Privacy is crucial. Avoid tracking people beyond what is necessary. Ask for consent to expand feature-sets. Or provide toggles and warnings. Avoid harm of people. Avoid dark patterns in software that manipulate brain patterns and reward systems.

### 7) Decision‑making & ownership

21. How do you make decisions when tradeoffs are messy?

  It is important to document the pros and cons of a decision, the context, and stance. Try to foresee waterfall effects after the decision is made. Document risks and mitigations.

22. How do you communicate mistakes or failures?

  I try to show what efforts were taken, document what was tried, and how it should have been done differently. Try to come up with a plan to solve the failure. 

23. What does “ownership” mean to you?

  Ownership means many things depending on context. Owning a product (as a manager) means having some kind of vision (final product, solving of pain points, use cases, etc) and getting involved to implement the vision. Owning a feature (as a developer) means to understand a problem or pain point and understand how it is being solved. Understand how the feature does its function and how to work on it. Taking ownership of a mistake (as a person) means admitting there was a personal fault and working to find a solution if it exists, and to prevent problems like this in the future. 

### Compression guidance (v2)

When compressing into the behavioral principles file, keep the core meaning but add a bit more variety and alternate phrasing. Avoid repeating the same verbs across consecutive responses.

Suggested alternates:
- acknowledge/validate/recognize impact
- clarify/align expectations
- offer options/propose next steps/confirm next actions
- set boundaries/define scope
- coach/pair/mentor
- document risks/mitigations
- calm and respectful/direct but kind/curious and professional

For leadership scope wording, prefer: "comfortable leading small teams; for larger groups (e.g., ~20), prefers a co-leader and clear delegation/ownership."
